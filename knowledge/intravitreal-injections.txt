TITLE: Intravitreal Injections

OVERVIEW
An intravitreal injection is a medical procedure in which medication is injected directly into the vitreous cavity, the jelly-filled space in the back of the eye. This procedure is typically performed by a retina specialist in an office setting and is commonly used to treat several retinal diseases. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

The vitreous cavity contains a clear gel called the vitreous humor. Delivering medication directly into this space allows drugs to reach the retina more effectively than systemic treatments.

WHAT ARE INTRAVITREAL INJECTIONS USED FOR?
Intravitreal injections are used to treat many retinal conditions, including:

- Age-related macular degeneration (AMD)
- Diabetic retinopathy
- Retinal vein occlusion
- Macular edema
- Eye infections such as endophthalmitis
- Retinitis

In some cases, a small gas bubble may be injected into the eye to help repair a retinal detachment.

MEDICATIONS USED IN INTRAVITREAL INJECTIONS
Several types of medications can be administered through intravitreal injection:

Anti-VEGF medications  
These drugs reduce abnormal blood vessel growth and leakage in the retina.

Steroids  
Steroid medications reduce inflammation and swelling in the eye.

Antibiotics, antivirals, and antifungal medications  
These medications are used to treat infections within the eye.

THE INJECTION PROCEDURE
Intravitreal injections are usually performed while the patient is reclining in a chair.

Typical steps in the procedure include:

1. Numbing the eye using anesthetic drops or gel.
2. Cleaning the eye and eyelids with povidone-iodine to reduce infection risk.
3. Placing an eyelid speculum to keep the eyelids open.
4. Injecting medication through the pars plana (the white part of the eye) using a very small needle.

Patients usually feel pressure during the injection but minimal pain. The entire procedure typically takes **10 to 15 minutes**. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

SAFETY AND RISKS
Intravitreal injections are generally very safe. Severe complications are rare, but potential risks include:

- Infection inside the eye (endophthalmitis)
- Inflammation in the eye
- Bleeding inside the vitreous gel (vitreous hemorrhage)
- Retinal detachment

A small surface bleed called a subconjunctival hemorrhage may occur where the needle entered the eye. This usually resolves within about one week.

Your retina specialist may also check the **intraocular pressure (IOP)** after the injection, as the pressure inside the eye may temporarily increase.

WHAT TO EXPECT AFTER THE INJECTION
Most patients can resume normal activities after the injection, although it is recommended to avoid contaminating the eye on the same day.

Patients should contact their retina specialist if they experience:

- Eye pain or discomfort
- Increased floaters
- Increased sensitivity to light
- Decreased vision

Some patients may feel as if something is in their eye following the procedure. This sensation is often due to irritation from the povidone-iodine cleaning solution. Artificial tears may help relieve this irritation.

REPEATED INJECTIONS
Many retinal diseases require **repeated intravitreal injections** over time. Chronic conditions such as:

- Age-related macular degeneration
- Diabetic macular edema
- Retinal vein occlusions

often require ongoing injections every **4–6 weeks** or as determined by the retina specialist.

Advances in anti-VEGF medications have made intravitreal injections one of the most important treatments for retinal disease today.

CLINICAL TERMS

Anti-VEGF Medications  
Drugs that block vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), reducing abnormal blood vessel growth and leakage in the retina.

Endophthalmitis  
A severe infection inside the eye that can cause pain, vision loss, and inflammation.

Fluorescein Angiography (FA)  
A diagnostic imaging technique that uses fluorescent dye to visualize blood flow in the retina.

Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)  
A non-invasive imaging test that produces detailed cross-section images of the retina.

Retinal Detachment  
A condition in which the retina separates from the back wall of the eye, potentially leading to vision loss.
