TITLE: Diabetic Retinopathy

OVERVIEW
Diabetic retinopathy is a complication of diabetes that damages the blood vessels of the retina, the light-sensitive tissue lining the back of the eye responsible for vision.

It is the most common cause of irreversible blindness in working-age adults in the United States.

More than half of people with diabetes eventually develop diabetic retinopathy. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

CAUSES
Diabetic retinopathy occurs when high blood sugar levels damage the small blood vessels that supply the retina.

These damaged blood vessels may:

- Leak fluid into the retina
- Become blocked
- Grow abnormal new blood vessels

Fluid leakage in the center of the retina (the macula) causes swelling called **diabetic macular edema (DME)**.

As the disease progresses, reduced circulation in the retina can trigger the growth of abnormal blood vessels, a process called **neovascularization**.

This advanced stage is known as **proliferative diabetic retinopathy (PDR)**.

New abnormal blood vessels may:

- Bleed into the eye
- Form scar tissue
- Pull on the retina and cause retinal detachment
- Block fluid drainage and cause high eye pressure (glaucoma)

All of these complications can lead to vision loss.

RISK FACTORS
Anyone with diabetes is at risk of developing diabetic retinopathy.

Risk increases with:

- Longer duration of diabetes
- Poor blood sugar control
- High blood pressure
- High cholesterol levels
- Pregnancy

SYMPTOMS
Diabetic retinopathy may develop for years without noticeable symptoms.

Symptoms may include:

- Blurred or distorted vision
- Difficulty reading
- Problems with color vision
- Spots or floaters in vision
- A shadow or dark area across the field of vision

Symptoms may occur in one or both eyes.

DIAGNOSTIC TESTING

Dilated Eye Examination
The most important test for diagnosing diabetic retinopathy is a dilated eye exam, which allows the doctor to closely examine the retina.

During this exam the physician looks for:

- Swelling of the retina (diabetic macular edema)
- Abnormal blood vessels
- Areas of poor blood circulation

Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)
OCT imaging provides detailed cross-sectional images of the retina and helps determine if fluid has leaked into retinal tissue.

Fundus Photography
Photographs of the retina help document the disease and monitor progression over time.

Fluorescein Angiography (FA)
In this test, dye is injected into a vein in the arm and travels through the eye's blood vessels. Special photographs detect areas where blood vessels are blocked, leaking, or abnormal.

TREATMENT
Several treatments are available to manage diabetic retinopathy.

Intravitreal Injections
Medications injected into the eye can block vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), reducing abnormal blood vessel growth and fluid leakage.

Common anti-VEGF medications include:

- Ranibizumab (Lucentis®)
- Bevacizumab (Avastin®)
- Aflibercept (Eylea®)
- Faricimab (Vabysmo®)

Steroid injections may also be used in certain cases.

Laser Treatment
Laser therapy called **panretinal photocoagulation (PRP)** is used to treat proliferative diabetic retinopathy.

Vitrectomy Surgery
In advanced cases, surgery may be required to remove blood from the eye or repair traction on the retina.

PREVENTION
People with diabetes can reduce their risk of diabetic retinopathy by managing their overall health.

Recommended strategies include:

- Taking medications as prescribed
- Maintaining a healthy weight
- Exercising regularly
- Controlling blood sugar levels (A1C)
- Controlling blood pressure and cholesterol
- Avoiding smoking

Regular eye exams are essential to detect the disease early and prevent vision loss.

PROGNOSIS
Early detection and treatment can prevent severe vision loss in many patients.

Because diabetic retinopathy can progress without symptoms, regular eye examinations are critical for people with diabetes.

CLINICAL TERMS

Diabetic Macular Edema (DME)
Swelling of the macula caused by fluid leaking from damaged blood vessels.

Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy (PDR)
An advanced stage of diabetic retinopathy characterized by abnormal blood vessel growth and scar tissue formation.

Neovascularization
Growth of abnormal new blood vessels due to poor blood circulation in the retina.

Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)
A non-invasive imaging technique used to produce detailed cross-sectional images of the retina.

Fluorescein Angiography (FA)
An imaging test using fluorescent dye to evaluate blood flow in the retina.

Intravitreal Injection
A medical procedure where medication is injected directly into the vitreous cavity of the eye.
