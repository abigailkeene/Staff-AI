TITLE: Leber Congenital Amaurosis (LCA)

OVERVIEW
Leber congenital amaurosis (LCA) refers to a group of inherited diseases that cause severe vision loss beginning in infancy. The condition results from abnormal function of the retina, the light-sensitive tissue at the back of the eye that captures images and sends signals to the brain.

In LCA, the retina’s photoreceptor cells (the light-sensing cells) do not function properly, preventing the eye from accurately capturing visual images. Over time, degeneration of the retina may occur, leading to progressive vision loss. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

CAUSES AND RISK FACTORS
LCA is a genetic condition passed down from parents through inherited gene mutations.

Key facts include:

- At least 19 different gene mutations can cause LCA.
- These genes normally produce proteins necessary for normal vision.
- The condition is typically inherited in an **autosomal recessive pattern**, meaning a child must inherit one abnormal gene from each parent.

Most parents who carry the gene mutation do not have vision loss themselves and may be unaware they are carriers.

Some gene mutations associated with LCA may also affect other organs or cause developmental delays, so children diagnosed with LCA may require evaluation by specialists familiar with inherited disorders.

SYMPTOMS
LCA is usually recognized during infancy or early childhood due to severe visual impairment.

Common signs include:

- Difficulty focusing on objects
- Lack of eye tracking when objects move
- Poor visual response from birth
- Frequent eye rubbing (which may stimulate the retina and produce flashes of light)

In some forms of LCA, vision problems may not appear until months or years after birth.

One subtype caused by mutations in the **RPE65 gene** often affects **night vision first**, especially in dim lighting. Over time, vision can worsen under all lighting conditions.

DIAGNOSTIC TESTING
The diagnosis of LCA involves several specialized eye tests.

An ophthalmic examination may initially show a retina that appears relatively normal, since degeneration develops later.

The most important diagnostic test is an **electroretinogram (ERG)**. This test measures electrical activity in the retina.

Patients with LCA typically show a **“flat” ERG**, meaning the retina demonstrates little or no electrical response to light stimulation.

Additional testing may include:

- Genetic testing to identify the specific gene mutation
- Genetic counseling for families
- Evaluation by pediatric specialists if systemic genetic disorders are suspected

TREATMENT
Historically, there has been no cure for LCA. However, recent advances in **gene therapy** have created treatment options for some patients.

One gene therapy treatment targets mutations in the **RPE65 gene**.

The therapy uses a medication called **voretigene neparvovec-rzyl (Luxturna™)**. This treatment delivers a functional copy of the defective gene directly into retinal cells.

The therapy is administered through a surgical procedure involving a **vitrectomy**, followed by injection of the gene therapy beneath the retina.

Clinical trials have shown that this treatment can improve vision in some patients, particularly their ability to navigate in low-light conditions.

Although gene therapy does not fully restore normal vision, improvements may significantly enhance quality of life.

The FDA has approved Luxturna™ for patients **1 year of age or older** who have mutations in both copies of the RPE65 gene and sufficient remaining retinal cells.

Other developing treatments include retinal prosthetic implants designed to bypass non-functioning photoreceptor cells.

Low-vision specialists can also help patients use assistive technologies to improve daily functioning.

PROGNOSIS
Without treatment, LCA typically leads to progressive vision loss and may result in severe visual impairment or blindness.

Early diagnosis is important because emerging treatments, particularly gene therapy, may help preserve or improve vision in some patients.

CLINICAL TERMS

Electroretinogram (ERG)
A non-invasive test that measures electrical responses of retinal cells to light stimulation.

Photoreceptors
Specialized cells in the retina that detect light and convert it into signals for the brain.

Retinitis Pigmentosa (RP)
A group of inherited retinal diseases that cause gradual degeneration of photoreceptor cells and progressive vision loss.
