TITLE: Branch Retinal Vein Occlusion (BRVO)

OVERVIEW
Branch retinal vein occlusion (BRVO) occurs when a vein in the retina becomes blocked, preventing normal blood flow from carrying oxygen and nutrients to retinal nerve cells.

Retinal vein occlusions occur when blood cannot properly drain from the retina.

Types of retinal vein occlusion:
- Central retinal vein occlusion (CRVO): blockage in the retina’s main vein
- Branch retinal vein occlusion (BRVO): blockage in a smaller branch vein

BRVO typically affects only a section of the retina.

CAUSES
Most BRVO cases occur at an arteriovenous crossing, which is where a retinal artery and vein intersect.

These vessels share a common connective tissue sheath. When the artery becomes stiff due to atherosclerosis (hardening of the arteries), it can compress the vein.

This compression causes:
- Turbulent blood flow
- Increased clot formation
- Vein blockage (occlusion)

The blockage prevents normal blood drainage and can lead to:

Macular Edema
Swelling in the central retina (macula) caused by fluid leakage.

Ischemia
Reduced blood flow to retinal tissue.

RISK FACTORS
Common risk factors for BRVO include:

- Uncontrolled high blood pressure
- Being overweight or obese
- Cardiovascular disease
- Glaucoma

In younger patients, an increased tendency for blood clotting may also contribute.

SYMPTOMS
BRVO usually causes sudden, painless vision loss.

If the affected area does not involve the macula (center of vision), the condition may go unnoticed.

Possible symptoms include:

- Sudden blurred or decreased vision
- Visual floaters
- Vision changes in one eye

Floaters may occur if abnormal blood vessels bleed into the vitreous gel inside the eye.

DIAGNOSTIC TESTING
BRVO is usually diagnosed during an eye examination.

Signs seen during exam may include:
- Retinal hemorrhage (bleeding in the retina)
- Thickened or twisted retinal blood vessels
- Retinal edema (swelling)

Two important imaging tests help confirm diagnosis:

Fluorescein Angiography (FA)
This test uses fluorescent dye to visualize retinal blood flow and identify leaking or blocked vessels.

Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)
OCT produces detailed images of the retina and helps detect macular edema and fluid accumulation.

Fluorescein angiography can demonstrate:

- Venous stasis (slowed blood flow)
- Edema (fluid swelling)
- Ischemia (reduced blood supply)
- Retinal neovascularization (growth of abnormal blood vessels)

TREATMENT AND PROGNOSIS

Initial Evaluation
Treatment begins by identifying and managing underlying medical conditions such as high blood pressure or cholesterol problems.

Medical evaluation may include:
- Blood pressure monitoring
- Cholesterol and lipid testing
- Blood tests to evaluate clotting disorders

Eye Treatments
Treatment focuses on complications of BRVO rather than removing the blockage itself.

Macular edema is the most common cause of vision loss in BRVO.

Anti-VEGF Injections
Macular edema is commonly treated with intraocular injections of anti-VEGF medications.

These medications reduce leakage from blood vessels and limit abnormal blood vessel growth.

Common anti-VEGF medications include:

- Avastin (bevacizumab)
- Lucentis (ranibizumab)
- Eylea (aflibercept)

Many patients experience significant vision improvement with treatment, although repeated injections may be required.

Laser Treatment
Laser therapy may be used in difficult cases.

Laser treatment for macular edema applies small laser pulses to the macula in a grid pattern.

Studies show that approximately two-thirds of patients experience vision improvement after several years.

Steroid Treatment
Steroid injections or implants may be used when anti-VEGF medications are not effective.

A slow-release steroid implant (dexamethasone or Ozurdex) has shown significant visual improvement in about 30% of patients.

Possible side effects of steroid treatment include:
- Increased eye pressure
- Cataract progression

COMPLICATIONS
A major complication of BRVO is retinal neovascularization.

This occurs when poor blood supply causes abnormal blood vessels to grow on the retina.

These abnormal vessels may cause:

- Vitreous hemorrhage
- Retinal detachment
- Glaucoma
- Vision loss

When neovascularization develops, scatter laser photocoagulation treatment may be used.

This laser treatment reduces the oxygen demand of the retina and helps stop abnormal blood vessel growth.

PROGNOSIS
BRVO generally has a favorable prognosis.

Some patients do not require treatment if the macula is not affected or if vision remains stable.

More than 60% of patients maintain vision better than 20/40 after one year.

CLINICAL TERMS

Anti-VEGF Drugs
Medications that block vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), reducing abnormal blood vessel growth and swelling in the retina.

Fluorescein Angiography (FA)
An imaging technique where fluorescent dye is injected into a vein to photograph circulation in the retina and choroid.

Floaters
Small moving spots or shadows seen in vision caused by debris or bleeding inside the eye.

Glaucoma
A condition where increased pressure in the eye damages the optic nerve.

Ischemia
Insufficient blood supply to tissue.

Macula
The central part of the retina responsible for detailed vision used for reading and recognizing faces.

Macular Edema
Swelling of the macula due to fluid accumulation.

Neovascularization
Abnormal growth of new blood vessels caused by poor oxygen supply.

Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)
A non-invasive imaging technique that creates cross-sectional images of the retina.

Retinal Detachment
A condition where the retina separates from the back of the eye.

Retinal Vein Occlusion
A blockage in retinal veins that prevents normal blood drainage from the retina.

Vitreous Hemorrhage
Bleeding into the vitreous gel that fills the eye.
