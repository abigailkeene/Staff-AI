# Acquired Vitelliform Lesions (AVL)
Source: American Society of Retina Specialists – Retina Health Series

## Overview
Adult-onset vitelliform maculopathy is a retinal disease characterized by round, yellowish deposits called **acquired vitelliform lesions (AVLs)** located beneath the central retina or macula.

Other names for this condition include:
- Adult pattern dystrophy
- Adult-onset foveomacular pigment epithelial dystrophy

These deposits form in the **subretinal space**, located between the retina and the retinal pigment epithelium (RPE).

## Retina Background
The retina is a thin layer of light-sensitive nerve tissue lining the back of the eye.

When light enters the eye:
1. Light passes through the iris.
2. Images are focused onto the retina.
3. The retina converts light into electrical signals.
4. Signals travel through the optic nerve to the brain.
5. The brain interprets these signals as vision.

## Symptoms
Patients with adult-onset vitelliform maculopathy may experience:

- Decreased vision
- Distorted vision

In mild cases, deposits may be discovered during an eye exam **before symptoms appear**.

## Causes and Risk Factors
Key facts about AVLs:

- Typically occur in adults in their **40s or 50s**
- Different from **Best vitelliform macular dystrophy**, which is inherited and appears earlier in life
- AVLs may occur alone or alongside **drusen**, which are yellow deposits associated with **age-related macular degeneration (AMD)**
- Deposits are located in the **subretinal space**
- Deposits consist primarily of **degenerating photoreceptor material**

## Vision Outcomes
Vision varies among patients:

- Nearly **half of patients maintain vision of 20/40 or better**
- Lesion size may correlate with vision loss
- Larger lesions are generally associated with decreased vision

AVLs **do not affect peripheral vision**.

## Diagnostic Testing

### Dilated Fundus Examination
A complete dilated eye exam is the first step in diagnosis.

### Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)
OCT is used to:

- Measure lesion size
- Monitor lesion progression
- Evaluate integrity of the outer retina

### Fluorescein Angiography (FA)
This test involves injecting a fluorescent dye into a vein to visualize retinal blood vessels and detect complications such as:

- Choroidal neovascularization (abnormal new blood vessels)

### Indocyanine Green Angiography (ICGA)
Another dye-based imaging technique used to evaluate blood flow in the choroid.

### OCT Angiography
A non-invasive imaging method used to detect abnormal blood vessel growth.

### Genetic Evaluation
Because vitelliform lesions can be associated with genetic disorders, doctors may perform:

- Family history review
- Electrophysiologic testing
- Genetic testing

## Treatment and Prognosis

### Treatment
AVLs must be differentiated from **neovascular age-related macular degeneration (AMD)**.

Important distinction:

- **AVLs generally do not require anti-VEGF injections**
- **Neovascular AMD often requires anti-VEGF treatment**

Correct diagnosis helps avoid unnecessary treatments.

### Prognosis
Some AVLs may resolve spontaneously.

However, resolution may be followed by:

- Retinal atrophy
- Reduced central vision

Peripheral vision typically remains unaffected.

## Clinical Terms

### Fluorescein Angiography (FA)
An imaging test where sodium fluorescein dye is injected into a vein, allowing retinal circulation to be photographed.

### Indocyanine Green Angiography (ICGA)
A diagnostic test using green dye to visualize blood flow in the **choroid**, the vascular layer beneath the retina.

### Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)
A non-invasive imaging technique that produces detailed cross-sectional images of the retina.

### Photoreceptors
Light-sensitive cells in the retina responsible for vision:
- **Rods** – night vision
- **Cones** – color vision and daylight vision

### Retinal Pigment Epithelium (RPE)
A pigmented cell layer beneath photoreceptors that supports retinal function and health.

## Key Takeaways
- AVLs are yellow deposits beneath the macula.
- Most commonly occur in adults in their 40s–50s.
- Symptoms include distorted or decreased vision.
- OCT imaging is critical for monitoring lesion progression.
- AVLs usually do **not require anti-VEGF injections** unless misdiagnosed as AMD.
- Peripheral vision is generally preserved.
