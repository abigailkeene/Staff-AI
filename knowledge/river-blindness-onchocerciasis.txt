TITLE: River Blindness (Onchocerciasis)

OVERVIEW
River blindness, also known as onchocerciasis, is a parasitic disease that can affect the eyes and cause severe vision loss or blindness. The disease is caused by infection with a parasitic worm transmitted by the bite of infected blackflies that live near fast-flowing rivers and fertile riverbanks. Once inside the body, the parasite can migrate through tissues, including structures within the eye, causing inflammation and damage that may eventually lead to blindness.

SYMPTOMS

Systemic (body-wide) symptoms may include:
- Intense itching that may begin in one area and spread across the body
- Skin nodules
- Skin rash
- Enlarged lymph nodes in the groin
- Changes in skin pigmentation

Eye-related symptoms may include:
- Eye itching
- Sensitivity to light (photophobia)
- Cataracts
- Inflammation inside the eye
- Progressive vision loss

CAUSES
River blindness occurs when humans are infected by parasitic worms transmitted through the bite of an infected blackfly. These flies live primarily along riverbanks where they breed.

After infection, the parasites can migrate through the body and reach the eye. The parasites may cause inflammation, bleeding, and damage to retinal and ocular tissues, eventually resulting in vision impairment or blindness if untreated.

RISK FACTORS
Onchocerciasis is most common in regions where infected blackflies are prevalent. It is a major cause of blindness in approximately 30 African countries, particularly in Western and Central Africa. The disease also occurs in Yemen and several countries in Latin America.

According to public health estimates, millions of people worldwide are affected by this parasitic infection.

DIAGNOSIS
Diagnosis may involve specialized laboratory testing to detect the parasite.

Common diagnostic methods include:

Skin Snip Biopsy  
A small sample of skin is taken and placed in saline solution. If parasite larvae are present, they emerge from the sample.

Blood Testing  
Blood samples may be tested for antibodies that indicate infection with the parasite.

These diagnostic tests help confirm infection and guide treatment.

TREATMENT
The primary treatment for river blindness is the anti-parasitic medication ivermectin.

Ivermectin works by killing the parasite larvae (microfilariae), reducing symptoms and preventing further damage to the eyes.

However, ivermectin does not kill adult worms. Because adult parasites may continue producing larvae, patients often need repeated treatment every 6 months until the infection has cleared.

PROGNOSIS
With proper treatment and monitoring, the progression of the disease can be controlled. Early treatment significantly reduces the risk of severe eye damage and blindness.

Regular medical follow-up is important to ensure the infection has fully resolved and to prevent recurrence of symptoms.
