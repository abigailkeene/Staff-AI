TITLE: Charles Bonnet Syndrome (CBS)

OVERVIEW
Charles Bonnet Syndrome (CBS) is a condition in which people with decreased vision experience visual hallucinations. These hallucinations occur in individuals with eye diseases or visual impairment but without psychiatric illness.

CBS is named after the 18th-century Swiss scientist Charles Bonnet, who first described the condition after observing it in his grandfather and later experiencing it himself.

The hallucinations may include simple patterns or complex images such as people, animals, buildings, or flowers. Importantly, individuals with CBS are typically aware that the images they see are not real.

SYMPTOMS
The primary symptom of Charles Bonnet Syndrome is visual hallucinations.

Characteristics of these hallucinations include:

- Occur while the person is awake
- Usually purely visual (not associated with sound)
- May include patterns, colors, people, animals, or objects
- Can appear suddenly and disappear without warning
- May recur over months or years

Many patients initially feel confused or concerned about these hallucinations. However, people with CBS generally understand that what they are seeing is not real.

The hallucinations may be pleasant, neutral, or occasionally disturbing, but they are usually not frightening.

Some individuals report seeing:

- People in their homes
- Animals or insects
- Patterns or colored grids
- Buildings or landscapes
- Detailed figures such as children wearing decorated clothing

CAUSES
The exact cause of CBS is not fully understood.

Most researchers believe the hallucinations occur due to reduced visual signals reaching the brain. When the brain receives less visual information from the eyes, spontaneous nerve activity in visual areas of the brain may produce images that are perceived as hallucinations.

This phenomenon is known as deafferentation, meaning a reduction or loss of sensory input from the eyes to the brain.

CBS occurs without cognitive or psychiatric disease.

RISK FACTORS
Several factors increase the likelihood of developing Charles Bonnet Syndrome:

- Visual impairment
- Retinal diseases such as macular degeneration
- Glaucoma
- Stroke affecting visual pathways
- Older age
- Social isolation
- Low-light environments

CBS is most common in people aged 80 and older, but it can occur at any age.

Approximately 10% to 30% of individuals with visual impairment affecting both eyes may experience CBS.

DIAGNOSTIC CONSIDERATIONS
Because hallucinations can also be caused by neurological or psychiatric disorders, physicians must rule out other causes before diagnosing CBS.

Other conditions that may produce hallucinations include:

- Parkinson’s disease
- Alzheimer’s disease
- Psychiatric illness
- Medication side effects
- Drug or alcohol withdrawal

A detailed patient history is very important in diagnosing CBS.

Patients with CBS do not have cognitive impairment and remain aware that the hallucinations are not real.

TREATMENT AND MANAGEMENT
There is no definitive medical treatment for Charles Bonnet Syndrome.

In many cases, hallucinations gradually decrease over time.

Management strategies may include:

- Improving lighting conditions
- Increasing social interaction
- Blinking or briefly closing the eyes when hallucinations occur
- Treating underlying eye diseases when possible

Education and reassurance are important components of care, as many patients fear they are developing mental illness.

PROGNOSIS
Hallucinations associated with CBS may persist for months or years but often become less frequent over time.

The condition itself does not indicate worsening eye disease.

Many patients learn to manage the hallucinations once they understand the cause.

CLINICAL TERMS

Visual Hallucination
A visual perception that appears real but occurs without external visual stimuli.

Formed Visual Hallucination
Hallucinations involving recognizable objects such as people, animals, or buildings.

Unformed Visual Hallucination
Hallucinations consisting of simple visual patterns, flashes of light, or colors.

Deafferentation
Loss or reduction of sensory signals from the eyes to the brain that may lead to spontaneous visual activity.
