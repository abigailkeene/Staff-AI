TITLE: Retinal Artery Occlusion

OVERVIEW
Retinal artery occlusion occurs when the artery supplying oxygen-rich blood to the retina becomes blocked. Because the retina requires constant oxygen to function, blockage of this artery can result in severe vision loss.

The retina is a thin layer of light-sensitive nerve tissue lining the back of the eye. It converts light entering the eye into electrical signals that travel through the optic nerve to the brain to produce vision.

Retinal artery occlusion is a serious condition and is considered an ocular emergency.

TYPES OF RETINAL ARTERY OCCLUSION

Central Retinal Artery Occlusion (CRAO)
CRAO occurs when the main retinal artery becomes blocked. This usually results in severe and sudden vision loss.

About 25% of people have an additional artery called a cilioretinal artery. If this artery is present and unaffected by the blockage, it may help preserve central vision.

Branch Retinal Artery Occlusion (BRAO)
BRAO occurs when a smaller branch artery becomes blocked. This may cause partial vision loss or loss of a portion of the visual field.

If the affected area is small or located outside the center of the retina, symptoms may be mild or go unnoticed.

SYMPTOMS
The most common symptom is sudden, painless vision loss in one eye.

Symptoms depend on which part of the retina is affected and may include:

- Sudden loss of vision
- Loss of part of the visual field
- Blurred vision
- Reduced central vision

CAUSES
Retinal artery occlusion is usually caused by blockage of the retinal artery by:

- Embolus (a small piece of cholesterol or debris that blocks blood flow)
- Thrombus (a blood clot)

The blockage may be temporary if the clot breaks apart and restores blood flow, or it may become permanent.

RISK FACTORS
Several medical conditions increase the risk of retinal artery occlusion:

- Carotid artery disease
- Atherosclerosis (fatty deposits in arteries)
- Heart valve disease
- Heart tumors such as myxoma
- Irregular heart rhythms (atrial fibrillation)
- Diabetes
- High blood pressure
- Intravenous drug use
- Giant cell arteritis
- Blood clotting disorders such as sickle cell disease
- Use of oral contraceptives
- Homocystinuria
- Pregnancy
- Platelet abnormalities

Most patients with retinal artery occlusion are in their 60s and the condition is more common in men than women.

Only about 1–2% of cases affect both eyes.

DIAGNOSTIC TESTING

Dilated Eye Examination
A retina specialist examines the retina using a dilated eye exam.

CRAO often shows a distinctive “cherry red spot,” where the macula appears red while surrounding retina becomes pale due to reduced blood flow.

BRAO typically appears as an area of retinal whitening along the blocked artery.

Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)
OCT imaging provides detailed cross-sectional images of the retina and shows swelling in the inner retinal layers in the affected region.

Fluorescein Angiography (FA)
Fluorescein angiography involves injecting fluorescent dye into a vein in the arm and photographing retinal circulation.

The test can show delayed filling of retinal arteries and help confirm the diagnosis.

TREATMENT AND MANAGEMENT
Unfortunately, there is currently no clinically proven treatment that reliably restores vision in central retinal artery occlusion.

Several emergency treatments may be attempted shortly after symptoms begin:

- Hyperventilation using carbogen (95% oxygen and 5% carbon dioxide)
- Paracentesis (removal of fluid from the front of the eye)
- Medications to lower intraocular pressure
- Ocular massage to attempt to dislodge the clot

These treatments must be attempted within approximately 4 to 6 hours of symptom onset to have any potential benefit.

Thrombolytic Therapy
Clot-dissolving medications have been studied but have not consistently shown benefit in clinical trials.

Systemic Evaluation
Because retinal artery occlusion shares many risk factors with stroke and cardiovascular disease, patients should undergo medical evaluation to identify underlying causes.

Common evaluations include:

- Carotid ultrasound to detect carotid artery plaque
- Cardiac echocardiography to evaluate heart valve disease
- Blood tests for inflammatory or clotting disorders

PROGNOSIS
Vision loss from CRAO is often severe and permanent.

However, patients with a cilioretinal artery may retain better central vision.

In cases of BRAO, visual field loss is often permanent but central vision may recover to 20/40 or better in many patients.

COMPLICATIONS
Rarely, abnormal blood vessels may develop on the retina or iris following arterial blockage.

These new vessels can cause additional complications including:

- Vitreous hemorrhage
- Glaucoma
- Further vision loss

Laser photocoagulation or anti-VEGF injections may be used to treat these complications.

CLINICAL TERMS

Embolus
A small piece of cholesterol or debris traveling in the bloodstream that can block an artery.

Thrombus
A blood clot that forms inside a blood vessel.

Macula
The central region of the retina responsible for detailed vision.

Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)
A non-invasive imaging technique used to create detailed images of the retina.

Fluorescein Angiography (FA)
An imaging test that uses fluorescent dye to visualize blood circulation in the retina.

Laser Photocoagulation
A laser treatment used to seal leaking blood vessels or treat abnormal vessel growth.

Vitreous Hemorrhage
Bleeding into the vitreous gel inside the eye.
