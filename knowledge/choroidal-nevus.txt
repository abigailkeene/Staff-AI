TITLE: Choroidal Nevus

OVERVIEW
A choroidal nevus (plural: nevi) is a pigmented lesion located in the back of the eye. It is similar to a freckle or mole on the skin and develops from pigment-containing cells in the choroid, the vascular layer beneath the retina and sclera.

Choroidal nevi are relatively common and often discovered during routine eye examinations.

EPIDEMIOLOGY
The estimated prevalence of choroidal nevi in the United States is approximately 5%, though the rate varies among different populations:

- White individuals: about 5.6%
- Hispanic individuals: about 2.7%
- Black individuals: about 0.6%
- Asian populations: approximately 1.8% to 2.9%

APPEARANCE
Choroidal nevi typically appear as brown to slate-gray spots with slightly blurred edges that blend into the surrounding retina.

Some nevi may appear:
- Nonpigmented (amelanotic), appearing yellowish rather than brown
- Associated with drusen (yellow deposits) on their surface
- Associated with degenerative changes in the retinal pigment epithelium (RPE)

Most nevi are flat or slightly elevated, usually measuring 2 millimeters or less in thickness.

SYMPTOMS
Most choroidal nevi do not produce symptoms and are discovered during routine eye examinations.

However, if a nevus is located beneath the macula (the center of the retina responsible for detailed vision), symptoms may occur, including:

- Blurred vision
- Distorted vision

In some cases, degeneration of the retinal pigment epithelium overlying the nevus can cause:

- Fluid accumulation beneath the retina
- Development of abnormal blood vessels (choroidal neovascularization)
- Leakage or bleeding that may lead to vision loss

DIAGNOSTIC TESTING

Eye Examination
Many nevi are identified based on their appearance during a dilated retinal examination.

Retinal Photography
Photographs of the lesion are commonly taken to monitor the nevus over time for any signs of growth.

Additional Imaging
For larger or suspicious lesions, additional diagnostic tests may include:

- Optical coherence tomography (OCT)
- Ultrasound imaging to measure lesion thickness
- Fluorescein angiography (FA)

Less commonly used imaging methods may include:

- Indocyanine green angiography
- Optical coherence tomography angiography (OCTA)
- Fundus autofluorescence imaging

RISK OF TRANSFORMATION
Most choroidal nevi remain benign (non-cancerous).

However, in rare cases a nevus may transform into uveal melanoma.

The estimated annual rate of transformation from choroidal nevus to melanoma is approximately 1 in 9,000 cases per year.

Signs that may indicate growth or malignant transformation include:

- Thickness greater than 2 mm
- Presence of subretinal fluid
- Visual symptoms such as flashes or floaters
- Orange pigment on the lesion
- Location close to the optic disc

TREATMENT AND MONITORING

Observation
Most nevi require no treatment and are monitored during routine eye exams.

Monitoring frequency depends on risk factors:

- Nevi without risk factors: annual examination
- Nevi with one or more risk factors: examination every 4 to 6 months

Treatment of Complications
If abnormal blood vessels develop beneath the retina, treatment may include intravitreal injections of anti-VEGF medications.

If a nevus demonstrates growth or suspicious features suggesting melanoma, referral to an ocular oncologist is recommended.

In cases where transformation into melanoma occurs, treatment typically involves radiation therapy.

PROGNOSIS
Most choroidal nevi remain stable and benign throughout life.

Regular monitoring ensures early detection if changes suggest malignant transformation.

CLINICAL TERMS

Choroid
The vascular layer of the eye located between the retina and sclera that supplies oxygen and nutrients to the retina.

Drusen
Yellow deposits that can form on the surface of a choroidal nevus or beneath the retina.

Retinal Pigment Epithelium (RPE)
A pigmented cell layer beneath the retina that supports retinal function.

Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)
A noninvasive imaging technique that produces detailed cross-sectional images of the retina.

Fluorescein Angiography (FA)
An imaging test using injected fluorescent dye to visualize retinal blood flow.

Intravitreal Injection
A medical procedure in which medication is injected directly into the vitreous cavity of the eye.

Neovascularization
Abnormal growth of new blood vessels that can leak fluid or bleed.

Uveal Melanoma
A malignant tumor that can develop from pigmented cells in the eye.
